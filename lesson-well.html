From the Well to the Witness

Jesus meets us in our broken places and turns our past into purpose.

Download Lesson PDF

Opening – Jesus at the Well

Today we sit with the Samaritan woman at the well – a woman who comes at the wrong time, in the heat of the day, carrying shame, routine and survival. She is not looking for a miracle. She is just trying to get through the day.

But Jesus is already on His way toward her. He chooses the long road through Samaria, a place others avoid, because she is worth the detour.

Bible Verse:
“Now Jesus learned that the Pharisees had heard that he was gaining and baptizing more disciples than John— although in fact it was not Jesus who baptized, but his disciples. So he left Judea and went back once more to Galilee.

Now he had to go through Samaria.” – John 4:1–4 (NIV)

This lesson reminds us:

  • Nothing in our past disqualifies us.
  • Jesus runs toward the broken, not away.
  • Encounters at ordinary places can lead to extraordinary callings.

The Story – From Hiding to Witness

1. The Setting – A Woman at the Wrong Place & Time

“When a Samaritan woman came to draw water, Jesus said to her, ‘Will you give me a drink?’” – John 4:7 (NIV)

Most women drew water in the early morning or evening – cool hours, communal moments. Coming at noon signals avoidance, shame and isolation. She chooses a time when no one else will be there.

Jesus intentionally travels through Samaria – a region Jews avoided because of deep religious and ethnic tension. He goes out of His way to meet her where she is hiding.

Important: Jesus goes out of His way to meet us in the places we hide.

Reflection
  • What places in your life feel like “noon wells,” where you go to avoid others?
  • How has Jesus met you in private places rather than public ones?

2. The Barriers – Why This Conversation Shouldn’t Happen

Three barriers should have prevented this encounter:

  • Race/Ethnicity: Jews vs Samaritans.
  • Gender: Men didn’t publicly speak to women like this.
  • Reputation: Her past shaped how others treated her.

Yet Jesus begins gently:

“Will you give me a drink?” – John 4:7 (NIV)

He leads with connection, not condemnation. He crosses every human boundary to sit with her in conversation.

Important: Jesus breaks barriers people use to keep others out.

Reflection
  • What barriers has Jesus crossed to reach you?
  • What barriers do you still put around yourself or others?

3. The Deeper Thirst – More Than Water

“But whoever drinks the water I give them will never thirst. Indeed, the water I give them will become in them a spring of water welling up to eternal life.” – John 4:14 (NIV)

She keeps returning to:

  • the well (routine)
  • relationships (comfort)
  • temporary solutions (survival)

Jesus shifts the conversation from physical water to living water – from outward need to inward thirst.

Important: We often try to satisfy spiritual thirst with temporary fixes.

Reflection
  • What “wells” do you keep returning to for satisfaction?
  • What are the things that temporarily relieve but never heal?

4. The Exposure – Truth Without Shame

“You are right when you say you have no husband. The fact is, you have had five husbands, and the man you now have is not your husband.” – John 4:17–18 (NIV)

Jesus speaks to her reality with gentleness. He knows her past, her pain and her patterns – and yet He does not step back.

Historical note: Multiple marriages didn’t always mean promiscuity. Women could not initiate divorce; they could be widowed, rejected or abandoned. Her story likely carries layers of grief and loss.

Important: Jesus already knows your story, and He still offers grace.

Reflection
  • How does Jesus speak truth to you?
  • Where do you still fear exposure or rejection?

5. The Revelation – Meeting the Messiah

“Then Jesus declared, ‘I, the one speaking to you—I am he.’” – John 4:26 (NIV)

This is one of the first clear declarations of Jesus as Messiah – and He gives it not to a religious leader or respected teacher, but to a Samaritan woman with a complicated past.

Important: Jesus reveals Himself to the open, not the perfect.

Reflection
  • Why do you think Jesus chose her for this revelation?
  • What does it say about who He values?

6. The Transformation – From Ashamed to Bold

“Then, leaving her water jar, the woman went back to the town and said to the people…” – John 4:28 (NIV)

Her response is instant. She leaves her water jar – the symbol of old habits, old identity and old survival – and runs back to the town she once avoided.

Important: An encounter with Jesus turns shame into testimony.

Reflection
  • What would it look like for you to “leave your water jar behind”?
  • What changed in her after meeting Jesus?

7. The Impact – Her Testimony Changed a City

“Many of the Samaritans from that town believed in him because of the woman’s testimony…” – John 4:39 (NIV)

Her past didn’t disqualify her – it qualified her. Her story, healed by Jesus, becomes the doorway for many others to believe.

Important: Your story, healed by Jesus, can bring others to Him.

Reflection
  • Whose faith could be unlocked by your testimony?
  • What story of yours might God be trying to use?

Closing Takeaway

Jesus doesn’t wait for perfection to call us. He meets us at the well, exposes our truth without shame, heals our identity, and turns our pain into purpose.

She came thirsty.
She left full.
She returned a witness.
